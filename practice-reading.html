The History of Tea

Tea is one of the most widely consumed beverages in the world, second only to water. Its origins can be traced back to ancient China, where, according to legend, it was discovered by the Emperor Shen Nong in 2737 BC when leaves from a wild tree blew into his pot of boiling water.

For centuries, tea remained a drink enjoyed primarily in Asia. It was during the Tang Dynasty (618–907 AD) that tea drinking became a refined cultural practice, with poets and scholars praising its calming qualities. The first known book about tea, written by Lu Yu, described in detail how to cultivate, prepare and serve it.

Tea did not arrive in Europe until the early seventeenth century, brought by Dutch and Portuguese traders. In Britain, it quickly became fashionable among the wealthy, and by the eighteenth century it had spread to all levels of society, helped by falling prices and the growth of trade with India and Ceylon.

Today, tea is grown in more than forty countries, with the largest producers being China and India. It continues to play an important role in social rituals around the world, from the Japanese tea ceremony to the British afternoon tea.
